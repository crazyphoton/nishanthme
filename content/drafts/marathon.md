When I first started running over a decade ago, I gradually built up to 2-3k without stopping. As I built up to 5k+ it became a drive to overcome the frequent feet pain I had because of my flat feet. I invested in good shoes, then built up to 10k. Eventually, I thought now that I have come so far, wouldn't it be cool to run a half marathon? I have finished 4 since then, in the 2:20-2:40 range. I have generally been completely flattened by the experience. The stretch after 18k was particularly brutal. Blisters on my feet, I was summoning every gram (no, I won't say ounce) of willpower I had to trot-walk-drag my way to the finish line.

Last year was different. I got a bit savvier with my quest for healthspan optimisation, and decided to train smarter. I did a lot of strength training and built up my general fitness. It was the first time I sped up in the last km and sprinted across the finish line. I felt I could run more if I hadn't done that last burst, and the folks who were embarking on a SECOND half marathon to finish 42.2km didn't seem absolutely insane.

Since then I have gotten it into my head that I can run the full 42.2k.

We all know running improves cardiorespiratory fitness, which is one of the strongest measurable predictors of long-term health; people with high versus low fitness had about half the mortality risk (HR 0.47, 95% CI 0.39-0.56) in a [2024 overview of meta-analyses spanning 199 cohort studies](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38599681/). But most of this fitness can be achieved at much lower levels of cardio than running a marathon.

On the other hand, athletes show a higher rate of atrial fibrillation. A [2021 systematic review and meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34253538/) found about 2.5 times the odds compared with non-athlete controls (OR 2.46, 95% CI 1.73-3.51). The pattern is not the one I expected, though. The same paper found athletes under 55 at greater risk than those 55 and over, and mixed sports carrying more risk than endurance sports — the opposite of the middle-aged-endurance-guy story I had in my head. None of it says an ordinary recreational runner carries a career athlete's risk.

There is also always the background risk of injury and dehydration. Risk of cardiac arrest is slightly higher. It is hard to maintain muscle mass with that much running.

So does running a marathon make you healthier, or are you worse off for attempting it? I spoke to a doctor friend trained in sports medicine, and he advised me that if planned properly, most of the downside risk can be almost eliminated — leaving a small net upside (I might lose a little weight and slightly improve my cardiovascular markers) and a temporary downside that can be reclaimed.

I will document my journey, along with my misconceptions and how I evaluated the risks and trade-offs, to illustrate how hard it can be to calibrate healthspan with a goal in mind.
